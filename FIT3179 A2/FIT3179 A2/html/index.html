Introduction

Queensland is home to the largest population of koalas in Australia. However, the koala population in Queensland has been rapidly declining due to a combination of habitat loss, urban development, climate change, and diseases. Car accidents and dog attacks also contribute to high mortality rates, especially in areas where urbanization has encroached on koala habitats. Conservation efforts are underway to protect koalas in Queensland, including habitat restoration, the creation of wildlife corridors, stricter regulations on land clearing, and medical treatment programs to combat diseases.

Both NGOs and government organisations, are working to preserve Queensland's koala populations through research, community engagement, and rescue operations. Despite these efforts, the species remains vulnerable, and continued action is essential to ensure the survival of koalas in Queensland. This data visualisation explores the current state of koalas in Queensland, the threats they face, and the conservation efforts being made to protect them.

In captivity

In some cases, koalas are found in captivity in wildlife rescue centers, zoos, or rehabilitation facilities. These koalas may be rescued due to injury, disease, habitat destruction, or other threats. They are often brought into captivity for treatment and conservation efforts, with the aim of returning them to the wild when they are healthy. Captive koalas are generally housed in wildlife sanctuaries, rehabilitation centers, or zoos in Queensland where they receive medical care, food, and shelter. (Wildlife Habitat, 2024)

In trees

Koalas are arboreal (tree-dwelling) animals, meaning they spend most of their time in trees. They are primarily found in eucalyptus forests and woodlands throughout Queensland, where they are well-adapted to live in the upper canopy. Trees are a good source of food, shelter and safety for koalas to rest and sleep.

On the ground

Koalas are not generally ground-dwelling animals, but they occasionally come down from trees to move between locations, typically to find new trees, mates, or water sources. They are very slow movers on the ground usually prefer to stay in trees to avoid predators and other threats. Koalas are vulnerable to cars, predators, and habitat destruction when they are on the ground. This is why habitat fragmentation (e.g., urban development) is such a threat to koalas. (Redland City Council, 2024)

Dog attacks, vehicle collisions and other threats

This highlight the human impact on koala populations, with many koalas suffering from these, possibly due to the dangers of urban environments and human-wildlife conflict. Urbanization and road development are posing a significant threat to koala survival, especially as they try to navigate fragmented habitats. Vehicle hits are a common cause of injury and death for koalas, particularly when they attempt to cross roads in human-inhabited areas. (Australian Koala Foundation, 2020)

Wasted and orphaned koalas

The presence of wasted and orphaned koalas points to a broader problem, which may include poor nutrition or loss of access to adequate habitats and food sources. Orphaned koalas represent a significant concern, as it indicates the loss of young koalas due to various reasons, including loss of their mothers.

Conservation efforts towards the koala population in Queensland


How effective are the efforts of government bodies and NGOs?

Rescue, rehabilitation, and release are essential to koala conservation efforts in Queensland. Koalas are vulnerable to threats like dog attacks, vehicle accidents, and diseases, and they rely on a network of dedicated volunteers who respond to community reports of koalas in need of help.

The race to rescue koalas

Koala rehabilitation begins with rescuing sick, injured, or orphaned koalas, a task typically carried out by trained and licensed volunteers with expertise in handling these animals. After being rescued, the koalas are taken to a wildlife hospital, clinic, or rehabilitation center. There, veterinarians assess their condition, checking for injuries or illness, and determine whether the animals can be treated and eventually released back into their natural habitat (Queensland Government, 2023).

What's next for Queensland's koala population?

Queensland Koala Conservation Strategy

As part of South-East Queensland's Koala Conservation Strategy (2020-2025), it focuses It focuses on habitat protection, reducing threats, enhancing wildlife corridors, and improving koala health monitoring. The strategy also aims to engage communities and stakeholders in conservation efforts, emphasizing the need for long-term, coordinated action to secure a future for koalas.

The Queensland Government also aims to work with various partners, including local councils, conservation groups, and wildlife organizations, to develop an implementation plan for koala conservation. This plan will involve activities like citizen science, public awareness campaigns, and contributing data to koala mapping efforts. Communities can actively participate in these initiatives to support koala preservation, either individually, or as part of NGO efforts. (Queensland Government, 2020)
